[[LG-2-1]]
==== LG 2-1: Understand the impact of AI on the lifecycle of architectural decisions

Participants understand the phases of an architectural decision lifecycle, from problem identification to eventual deprecation. They understand that this lifecycle is cyclical rather than linear and can explain how agentic engineering affects this lifecycle (effects, risks and opportunities).
In detail, participants: 

* understand the phases of the decision lifecycle: problem identification, option exploration, (in)validation of alternatives, decision making, decision establishment, deprecation, and iteration
* understand the effect of agentic engineering: It accelerates exploration and (prototype) development, and - paired with continuous validation - increases decision cycle frequency
* understand that it is important to establish feedback loops from development, testing, and operations into ongoing architectural reasoning
* can explain how these effects make it possible to establish a continuous, evidence based architecture process, and why this is a good thing.

[[LG-2-2]]
==== LG 2-2: Identify architectural drivers and structural pain points (bottom-up)

Participants are able to identify architectural drivers and structural pain points by using a mixture of established tools and AI-assisted techniques. They understand how findings from analysis and reviews can be used to focus architectural work and inform architectural decision-making.
In detail, participants: 

* can explain bottom-up needs for architectural work, especially in agentic engineering contexts: structural weaknesses, unintended drift, or emerging complexity in evolving systems
* know established tools: technical debt assessment, static structural analysis, monitoring-based feedback and dynamic analysis, metrics and test coverage analysis, architecture reviews
* can use AI-assisted techniques to support bottom-up driver identification: like AI-driven architecture reviews and structural assessments, consistency checks and quality assessments

[[LG-2-3]]
==== LG 2-3: Refine quality goals into architectural evaluation criteria (top-down)

Participants are able to decompose quality goals into concrete evaluation criteria and use them to reason about the output of agentic development.

* Decomposing quality goals into concrete, assessable criteria
* Identifying conflicts or contradictions between quality goals, constraints, and architectural intentions
* Using quality criteria to assess evolving solutions against intended architectural qualities (see chapter 4)

[[LG-2-4]]
==== LG 2-4: Use AI for design exploration and architectural variant generation

Participants are able to use AI to support the exploration of architectural options and the generation of architectural variants. They understand how candidate options can be examined in relation to architectural drivers, constraints, existing decisions, and architectural principles.

* Generating and refining architectural variants for a given problem situation
* Conducting AI-assisted trade-off analysis across architectural alternatives
* Identifying decision forces and evaluating candidate options against existing decisions, constraints, and architectural principles
* Understanding tool-independent decomposition and evaluation techniques as a basis for critically assessing AI-generated suggestions
* Especially for structural design questions: Identifying fracture planes by augmenting static analysis with AI-generated structural insights

[[LG-2-5]]
==== LG 2-5: Being able to treat architectural decisions as testable hypotheses

Participants understand that architectural decisions can be framed as explicit hypotheses. They are able to design lightweight experiments to obtain early feedback and reduce uncertainty in architectural decision-making.

* Framing architectural decisions as hypotheses with assumptions, expected outcomes, and measurable criteria for validation or invalidation.
* Replacing potentially existing meeting-based approaches for architectural design with experimental loops
* Designing lightweight experiments such as spikes, prototypes, and side-by-side variant comparisons
* Identifying architectural risks and using targeted experiments to address them
* Using experimental outcomes to support, weaken, revise, or revisit architectural decisions

[[LG-2-6]]
==== LG 2-6: Understand the limitations of AI in architectural decision support

Participants understand the limitations of AI in architectural reasoning and the support of decision-making. They know why AI-generated results require critical review and understand the need to retain human responsibility for consequential architectural decisions.

* Known risks include model-inherent aspects that need attention: hallucinations, bias, sensitivity to prompt structure and instruction ordering, differences in context effectiveness, potentially sponsored technologies, ...
* Participants know the value and risks of switching models during a decision process
* Participants are able to define review checkpoints and know how to maintain human responsibility over consequential architectural decisions
